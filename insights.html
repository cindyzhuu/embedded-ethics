Research Insights

Key findings from 10 interviews with educators on effectively incorporating embedded ethics into AP Computer Science A

Study Overview

Across both Harvard Embedded EthiCS interviews and high school AP CS teacher interviews, we identified consistent patterns in how students engage with ethics education. Our research reveals structural challenges and practical strategies for making ethics meaningful in computer science education.

Key Research Findings

Ethics as Peripheral vs. Core

Finding: Across both Harvard Embedded EthiCS and high school AP CS interviews, students tend to treat ethics as peripheral unless it is clearly tied to technical work, real decisions, or graded course expectations.

Problem: Ethics is often positioned as something to discuss around computing rather than something embedded within computing practice. Students can ignore ethics material and still succeed in the course.

Current ethics in AP CS Principles doesn’t get into CS technically but rather technology as a whole — accessibility is a big one, safe use of data, keeping it secure, simple ways of how to protect data, what might be shared and how it can be.”
- AP CS Principles Teacher

"Guest Lecture" Effect

Finding: Ethics modules often feel like a "guest lecture" or side conversation rather than part of the real intellectual work of the course.

Evidence: At Harvard, students sometimes skip class when they know an Embedded EthiCS module is coming. In high school, students see ethics activities as "not real coding" or separate from what they signed up to learn.

"Students often see ethics activities as 'not real coding' or separate from what they signed up to learn."
- AP CS A Teacher

Engagement Rises When Ethics Looks Like CS

Finding: Students engage more when ethics is presented through technical tradeoffs, design choices, and concrete cases rather than abstract moral discussion.

Success Factors: Algorithmic design tradeoffs, mathematical or quantitative reasoning, real engineering scenarios with no obvious right answer, questions tied to students' likely future work in tech.

"What counts as “interesting” is topical to the course. The worst case scenario is where the philosophical concept is not grounded in course material. We are not expecting concepts themselves to be the most intrinsically exciting thing. Something that students will feel is important. Phil can come off as self-evident, but (personally) hoping that the concepts themselves will stick with you, even if they’re basic. We’ve discussed that we’re hoping that as you go out into CS career, the takeaways will illuminate ethical reasoning you’ll encounter later on."
- Embedded EthiCS Team Member

AI Makes Ethics Urgent But Narrow

Finding: In AP CS classrooms, AI has made ethics feel newly unavoidable, but the focus has narrowed primarily to academic integrity and appropriate AI use.

Limitation: Ethics in high school CS is often discussed through misuse rather than deeper questions about system design, labor, incentives, or downstream societal effects.

"Gemini being embedded directly into Google Classroom makes the ethics of AI use inseparable from everyday instruction."
- AP CS A Teacher

Staffing and Expertise Shape Ethics Content

Finding: Ethics education often depends on who is available to teach it, creating disconnects between philosophical training and technical specificity.

Impact: At Harvard, modules sometimes drifted toward familiar philosophical thought experiments rather than authentic computing problems. At high schools, ethics content tends to be broad and external to programming itself.

"I think Embedded EthiCS needs to feel a bit more interdisciplinary...the people on the staff are mainly Philosophy people (without specific interests in technology). There'sa big collaboration problem...the process of module development feels not collaborative."
- Embedded EthiCS Team Member

Topics Should Draw from Students' Lived Concerns

Finding: Students are primarily motivated by learning to program, and while they remain engaged in class, their interest is concentrated on technical content rather than broader ethical implications.

Caveat: Because students often lack lived experience with the real-world consequences of data and technology, they may struggle to grasp the bigger picture or fully appreciate the ramifications of ethical issues.

“Main reason a lot of students take the class is for programming; they want to learn to program, so I don’t necessarily see students skipping class, but from an interest standpoint there’s definitely more interest in it — don’t think that their perspective; their perspective is still different; they don’t see the bigger picture when it comes to these things (age or experiences) like data — they haven’t been affected by it; don’t see the ramifications.”
- AP CS Principles Teacher

Core Takeaway

The core challenge in ethics education is less about persuading students that ethics matters than about restructuring CS learning so that ethics is experienced as part of real technical practice.

Across both Harvard and AP CS contexts, the same lesson holds: ethics becomes meaningful when it is concrete, recurring, tied to design, and required for success.

Principles for Effective Ethics Integration:

Concrete

Tie ethics to specific technical decisions and design choices

Recurring

Integrate ethics throughout the curriculum, not as isolated modules

Design-Focused

Frame ethics as part of engineering tradeoffs and constraints

Required

Make ethical reasoning essential for course success
